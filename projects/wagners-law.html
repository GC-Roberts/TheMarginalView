The Marginal View

Revisiting Wagner’s Law in South Africa (1960–2024) • Download

Giovanni C. Roberts · 2025

Abstract

This paper re-examines Wagner’s Law in a South African context over the period 1960–2024. Wagner’s Law suggests that as real national income increases, government final consumption expenditure expands more than proportionally due to a higher income elasticity of demand for collective goods.

Using annual data on real GDP and general government final consumption expenditure at constant 2015 prices, this study applies stationarity testing, autoregressive corrected estimation, and vector autoregression to assess both long-run elasticity and short-run causality. The findings contribute to the fiscal-growth literature in developing economies and inform South Africa’s fiscal policy challenges.

Introduction

The role of government in modern economies is extensive and multifaceted. Two dominant theoretical frameworks shape the debate on public expenditure and economic growth: Wagner’s Law and Keynesian theory.

While Wagner’s Law posits that government spending rises disproportionately as income grows, Keynesian theory asserts that public expenditure can act as an exogenous driver of economic activity. South Africa’s fiscal trajectory since 1960 provides an ideal empirical setting to evaluate these competing perspectives.

Theoretical Framework and Literature

Wagner’s Law rests on three foundational assumptions: expanding legal and administrative functions, rising externalities from industrialization, and high income elasticity of demand for public goods. Collectively, these imply an increasing government share as economies develop.

Empirical evidence remains mixed. While advanced economies often exhibit elasticity coefficients exceeding unity, developing economies display heterogeneous results due to institutional constraints and structural dynamics. For South Africa, prior studies report elasticity above one but ambiguous causality.

Data and Methodology

Annual observations from 1960–2024 on real GDP and government final consumption expenditure were obtained from the World Bank Development Indicators. Series were expressed in constant 2015 prices and transformed into natural logarithms.

Stationarity was assessed using the Augmented Dickey–Fuller test. Given evidence of autocorrelation, elasticity was estimated using an AR(1) corrected GLS model. A bivariate VAR framework was employed to examine Granger causality between fiscal expenditure and output.

Results

The estimated elasticity coefficient of government expenditure with respect to GDP is approximately 1.20, indicating strong support for Wagner’s Law. This suggests that public expenditure in South Africa rises more than proportionally with income over the long run.

Granger causality tests reveal that government expenditure significantly predicts future GDP, while GDP does not significantly predict government spending. This supports Keynesian dynamics in the short run, where fiscal policy acts as a catalyst for economic activity.

Policy Implications

Fiscal policy must balance long-term expenditure growth with short-term stabilization objectives. Strong elasticity implies that without corrective measures, government spending may outpace revenue growth.

During economic downturns, targeted fiscal interventions and automatic stabilizers play a crucial role in mitigating demand shocks. However, expenditure growth must be carefully calibrated to preserve fiscal sustainability and avoid crowding out private investment.

Conclusion

This study provides empirical support for Wagner’s Law in South Africa while also validating Keynesian causality in the short run. The coexistence of these dynamics underscores the complexity of the fiscal-growth nexus and highlights the need for integrated and context-sensitive fiscal strategies.
